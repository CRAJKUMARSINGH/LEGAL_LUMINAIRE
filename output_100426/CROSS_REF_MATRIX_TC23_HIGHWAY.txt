## Oral Argument Paragraphs (Ready-to-Use — 5 Paras)

**Para 1 — Opening (78-Day Possession Delay):**
"My Lord, the Letter of Acceptance was issued on 10.06.2024. NHAI was contractually bound under FIDIC Sub-Clause 2.1 and NHAI GCC Clause 5 to give the Claimant right of access to the site within 15 days — by 25.06.2024. The site was not handed over until 12.09.2024 — a delay of 78 days. The reason is not disputed: NHAI's own Forest Department correspondence confirms that clearance for 8.2 km was pending. NHAI issued the LOA knowing this. The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Pramod Kumar has held that such delay entitles the contractor to full compensation. The Claimant's Claim No. 1 of ₹14.85 Crore is therefore fully justified."

**Para 2 — Contradiction: NHAI's Own Records Prove the Delay:**
"My Lord, NHAI's defence is that there was no delay in site possession. But NHAI's own Forest Department letters, Revenue records, and Police complaints regarding farmer protests — all produced by NHAI itself — confirm the 78-day delay. The Standing Committee Minutes of 12.02.2026 recommend termination but are conspicuously silent about NHAI's own possession delay. This silence is itself an admission. A party cannot blow hot and cold — NHAI cannot deny the delay while its own documents prove it."

**Para 3 — Blacklisting Illegal:**
"My Lord, NHAI has recommended 3-year blacklisting of the Claimant. The Supreme Court in Erusian Equipment v. State of West Bengal has held that blacklisting without a hearing violates Article 14 of the Constitution. No show-cause notice was issued before the blacklisting recommendation. No opportunity was given to the Claimant to respond. The blacklisting recommendation is unconstitutional and must be set aside. Further, the delay was caused by NHAI's own defaults — blacklisting a contractor for NHAI's own failure is perverse."

**Para 4 — FIDIC 20.2 Notice:**
"My Lord, NHAI may argue that the Claimant failed to give 28-day notice under FIDIC Sub-Clause 20.2. But 42 emails were sent between 15.06.2024 and 10.09.2024 — all on record. The Delhi High Court in Patel Engineering Ltd. v. NHAI has held that the notice requirement under FIDIC 20.2 is directory, not mandatory, when the Employer has actual knowledge of the claim. NHAI had actual knowledge — its own officers received and acknowledged the emails. The notice requirement is satisfied."

**Para 5 — Closing (Award Sought):**
"My Lord, the Claimant has suffered losses of ₹55.30 Crore due entirely to NHAI's defaults. Four of the five claims are VERIFIED with contemporary records scoring 82-96% on the Fact-Fit Gate. The fifth claim (loss of profit) is SECONDARY and we present it with appropriate qualification. We pray for an award of ₹55.30 Crore with interest at 12% per annum, declaration that the termination and blacklisting are illegal, release of the Performance Bank Guarantee of ₹5.63 Crore, and costs of arbitration."
