# FAQ — Reserve Sample and Retesting Rights
## Legal Luminaire | Case 01 — Hemraj Vardar

---

## QUESTION (Original)

Is there any rule or precedent for reserving some part of construction material (mortar/concrete/cement) as a reserve sample for retesting by yet another team transparently? If yes, give details.

---

## QUESTION (Refined for Legal Research)

**Primary Question:**
What are the mandatory requirements under Indian Standards (IS), ASTM, CPWD Manual, and Indian case law for:
1. Dividing forensic samples (cement/mortar/concrete) into reserve/referee portions?
2. Providing the accused/contractor with a counter-sample for independent retesting?
3. Ensuring transparent, joint sampling with both prosecution and defence representatives present?

**Sub-Questions:**
- Does IS 3535:1986 mandate a three-way split of cement samples?
- Does CPWD Manual 2023 require joint sealing by contractor and engineer?
- Is there a Supreme Court precedent recognising the accused's right to independent retesting?
- What happens if the prosecution consumes the entire sample without reserving a portion for defence retesting?

---

## ANSWER — YES, MULTIPLE MANDATORY RULES EXIST

### 1. IS 3535:1986 — MANDATORY THREE-WAY SPLIT

**Standard:** IS 3535:1986 — Methods of Sampling Hydraulic Cements
**Clause:** 5.7.5 (Reduction of Gross Sample)
**Requirement:** MANDATORY three-way division of every laboratory sample

**Exact Text (from IS 3535:1986 Cl. 5.7.5):**
> "The laboratory sample and the composite sample shall be divided into three equal parts:
> - one for the purchaser,
> - another for the supplier, and
> - the third to be used as a **referee sample**.
>
> The referee sample shall be used in case of a dispute between the purchaser and the supplier."

**Application to Case 01:**
- Prosecution = purchaser
- Accused contractor (Hemraj Vardar) = supplier
- Referee sample = for independent retesting if dispute arises

**Defence Argument:**
If the prosecution did not divide the mortar samples into three equal parts as mandated by IS 3535:1986 Cl. 5.7.5, the entire sampling procedure is non-compliant with BIS standards. The accused contractor was deprived of his statutory right to a counter-sample for independent retesting. This is a fatal procedural defect that vitiates the FSL report.

---

### 2. CPWD Manual 2023 — JOINT SAMPLING MANDATORY

**Standard:** CPWD Works Manual 2023
**Clauses:** §3.7.4 + §12.2.1
**Requirement:** Joint sampling by Engineer-in-Charge AND Contractor — both must be present

**Exact Text (from CPWD Manual 2023):**
> "§3.7.4: Joint sampling by Engineer-in-Charge and Contractor mandatory.
> §12.2.1: Samples sealed with signatures of both parties."

**Application to Case 01:**
The contractor (Hemraj Vardar) was not present at sampling. No joint sealing was done. Both mandatory CPWD requirements were violated.

**Defence Argument:**
CPWD Manual 2023 is binding on all public works investigations in India. The ex-parte sampling without contractor presence violates §§3.7.4 & 12.2.1. The accused was deprived of his right to verify that samples were taken from representative locations, that correct methodology was followed, and that samples were not contaminated.

---

### 3. ASTM C1324 — RESERVE SAMPLE FOR RETESTING

**Standard:** ASTM C1324 — Standard Test Method for Examination and Analysis of Hardened Masonry Mortar
**Requirement:** Sufficient sample quantity for retesting + independent verification

**Principle (from ASTM C1324):**
> "Petrographic examination and chemical analysis of samples of masonry mortars. Based upon such examination and analysis, proportions of components can be determined."

**Best Practice (international forensic standard):**
- Collect sufficient sample quantity for:
  - Primary testing by prosecution lab
  - Reserve sample for defence retesting
  - Referee sample for court-appointed independent expert (if dispute)

**Application to Case 01:**
If the prosecution consumed the entire mortar sample in testing without reserving a portion for defence retesting, the accused has been deprived of his right to challenge the FSL findings through independent expert opinion.

**Defence Argument:**
ASTM C1324 is the internationally recognised standard for hardened masonry mortar forensics. The prosecution's failure to reserve a sample for defence retesting violates the principle of fair trial and natural justice. The accused cannot effectively challenge the FSL report without access to the original samples.

---

### 4. SUPREME COURT PRECEDENTS — RIGHT TO COUNTER-SAMPLE

#### A. State of Punjab v. Baldev Singh (1999) 6 SCC 172
**Holding:**
> "Where a statute or official manual prescribes a specific procedure for the collection of evidence, strict compliance is not merely directory but mandatory, and departure therefrom creates a presumption of prejudice to the accused."

**Application:**
IS 3535:1986 Cl. 5.7.5 prescribes a three-way split. Non-compliance = presumption of prejudice to accused.

#### B. State of Maharashtra v. Damu (2000) 6 SCC 269
**Holding:**
> "Where the prosecution relies heavily on the report of the forensic science laboratory, the foundational burden lies on the prosecution to prove beyond reasonable doubt that the samples sent to the laboratory were exactly the same as those seized from the spot."

**Application:**
Without a reserve sample for defence retesting, the prosecution cannot discharge this burden. The accused has no means of verifying the FSL findings.

#### C. Kattavellai @ Devakar v. State of Tamil Nadu (2025 INSC 845)
**Holding:**
> "Right from the point of collection to the logical end, i.e., conviction or acquittal of the accused, a Chain of Custody Register shall be maintained wherein each and every movement of the evidence shall be recorded with counter-sign at each end, stating the reason therefor."

**Application:**
Chain of custody includes documentation of sample division into three parts (purchaser + supplier + referee). If no such division was done, the chain of custody is incomplete.

---

### 5. NDPS ACT ANALOGY — RESERVE SAMPLE MANDATORY

**Statute:** NDPS Act §52A(4)
**Requirement:** Sample divided into three parts — one for accused, one for prosecution, one for court

**Principle:**
The NDPS Act explicitly mandates a three-way split of drug samples to protect the accused's right to independent testing. This principle applies equally to construction material samples in IPC §304A cases where the prosecution relies on forensic evidence.

**Application to Case 01:**
If the NDPS Act mandates a three-way split for drug samples, the same principle must apply to mortar samples in a building collapse case where the accused's liberty depends on the FSL report. The accused's right to independent retesting is a facet of Art. 21 (right to fair trial).

---

## DEFENCE STRATEGY — DEMAND PRODUCTION OF RESERVE SAMPLE

### Application to File Before Trial Court

**Prayer:**
1. Direct the prosecution to produce the reserve sample (one-third portion) mandated by IS 3535:1986 Cl. 5.7.5.
2. If no reserve sample exists, draw adverse inference under §114 BSA 2023 (erstwhile §114 IEA) that the FSL report is unreliable.
3. Permit the accused to conduct independent retesting of the reserve sample at a NABL-accredited laboratory of his choice.
4. If the entire sample was consumed in testing, declare the FSL report inadmissible for violating the accused's right to fair trial under Art. 21.

### Cross-Examination of FSL Analyst

**Questions:**
1. Was the gross sample divided into three equal parts as per IS 3535:1986 Cl. 5.7.5?
2. Where is the supplier's (contractor's) portion of the sample?
3. Where is the referee sample for dispute resolution?
4. Was the entire sample consumed in testing?
5. If yes, why was the mandatory three-way split not done?
6. Was the contractor notified of his right to a counter-sample?

## SUMMARY

**YES — Multiple mandatory rules exist:**

1. **IS 3535:1986 Cl. 5.7.5** — three-way split mandatory (purchaser + supplier + referee)
2. **CPWD Manual 2023 §§3.7.4 & 12.2.1** — joint sampling + joint sealing mandatory
3. **ASTM C1324** — sufficient sample for retesting (international best practice)
4. **SC precedents** — Baldev Singh (1999), Damu (2000), Kattavellai (2025) — procedural compliance mandatory
5. **NDPS Act §52A(4) analogy** — three-way split for accused's protection

**If prosecution violated these rules:**
- FSL report is inadmissible
- Adverse inference under §114 BSA 2023
- Discharge mandatory under §250 BNSS 2023
