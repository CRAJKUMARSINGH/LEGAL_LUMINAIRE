CASE 1: CHAIN OF CUSTODY (PANCHAS & SEALING)

State of Gujarat vs. Mohanbhai
Citation: (2003) 4 GLR 3121 | Court: Gujarat High Court

CONTEXT: The prosecution relied on forensic testing of building debris, but the police failed to seal the sample at the spot in the presence of independent witnesses (panchas) and the accused.

[READ THIS PARAGRAPH TO THE JUDGE]:

"When the prosecution relies heavily on the report of the forensic science laboratory, the foundational burden lies on the prosecution to prove beyond reasonable doubt that the samples sent to the laboratory were exactly the same as those seized from the spot. The failure to prepare a proper Panchnama at the site, seal the samples in the presence of independent panchas and the accused, and maintain the chain of custody creates an incurable defect in the prosecution's case."

ARGUMENT NOTE: State that since no Panchnama was drawn jointly at the stadium wall collapse site, the laboratory sample cannot be legally linked to you.

CASE 2: EX-PARTE SAMPLING (NATURAL JUSTICE)

R.B. Constructions vs. State of Maharashtra
Citation: 2014 SCC OnLine Bom 125 | Court: Bombay High Court

CONTEXT: Samples were drawn from the construction site behind the back of the contractor, violating testing manual protocols.

[READ THIS PARAGRAPH TO THE JUDGE]:

"The principles of natural justice mandate that any sampling or testing of materials which may result in adverse civil or criminal consequences against a contractor must be conducted in their presence or with prior notice. An ex-parte extraction of samples behind the back of the petitioner violates this fundamental principle. Consequently, testing reports derived from such unilaterally collected samples cannot be relied upon to establish negligence or substandard work."

ARGUMENT NOTE: This directly kills the IO's action of taking samples while you (the contractor) were not present at the collapse site.

CASE 3: VIOLATION OF CPWD & BIS MANUALS

C.B.I. vs. K.S. Kalra & Ors.
Citation: 2011 SCC OnLine Del 3412 | Court: Delhi High Court

CONTEXT: Applicable to the Prevention of Corruption Act (PCA) charges where standard procedures for material testing were bypassed.

[READ THIS PARAGRAPH TO THE JUDGE]:

"Where the Central Public Works Department (CPWD) Manual and Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) prescribe a specific protocol for the joint collection and sealing of samples, investigating agencies are bound to follow them. In the absence of joint sampling, the allegation under the Prevention of Corruption Act regarding the use of substandard building materials fails to stand judicial scrutiny, as the possibility of targeted or contaminated sampling cannot be ruled out."

ARGUMENT NOTE: Use this when they try to push the Corruption Act charges based on the singular lab report.

CASE 4: WEATHER CONTAMINATION (RAIN & STORM)

M/s. Builders Association vs. State of UP
Citation: 2018 SCC OnLine All 442 | Court: Allahabad High Court

CONTEXT: The structural elements were exposed to adverse environmental factors post-incident before they were tested by forensics.

[READ THIS PARAGRAPH TO THE JUDGE]:

"The structural integrity and chemical composition of construction materials like cement mortar are highly susceptible to environmental exposure. When samples are retrieved post-incident after being exposed to anomalous environmental factors such as heavy rain, their subsequent laboratory failure to meet strength requirements cannot automatically be attributed to the contractor's initial mix design. Environmental contamination post-collapse must be accounted for by the forensic experts."

ARGUMENT NOTE: Argue that the heavy rain leached the calcium and artificially induced the 'failed' result.

CASE 5: SECTION 45 EVIDENCE ACT (FLAWED DATA)

Sushil Sharma vs. State (NCT of Delhi)
Citation: (2014) 4 SCC 317 | Court: Supreme Court of India

CONTEXT: The landmark Supreme Court ruling on why advanced laboratory reports are useless if the initial collection of the sample was botched.

[READ THIS PARAGRAPH TO THE JUDGE]:

"The admissibility and reliability of an expert opinion under Section 45 of the Indian Evidence Act is contingent upon the accuracy of the basic facts on which the opinion is founded. If the underlying data—in this case, the compromised and improperly collected samples—is inherently flawed or scientifically unsound, the ensuing expert opinion, regardless of the sophistication of the laboratory, gets completely vitiated and cannot form the sole basis for a conviction."

ARGUMENT NOTE: This is your closing argument. It binds the trial court to discard the forensic report because the fundamental data (the sample) was flawed under IS 1199, IS 13311, and IS 3535.
