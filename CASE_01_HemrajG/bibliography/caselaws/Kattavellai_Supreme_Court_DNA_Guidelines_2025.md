## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Indian criminal justice system witnessed a significant transformation on July 15, 2025, when the Supreme Court of India delivered a landmark judgment in Kattavellai @ Devakar vs State of Tamil Nadu, establishing comprehensive guidelines for the collection, preservation, and processing of DNA evidence. This watershed moment represents a critical evolution in forensic jurisprudence, addressing systemic gaps that have long plagued criminal investigations and potentially led to wrongful convictions.

## FACTUAL BACKGROUND

The case originated from a heinous crime in Tamil Nadu in 2011, involving the murder of a couple and the rape of the female victim. Kattavellai @ Devakar was sentenced to death by the trial court in 2018, with the conviction resting almost entirely on circumstantial evidence, primarily DNA matches between biological samples collected from the crime scene and the accused. The Madras High Court subsequently affirmed this conviction.

## CRITICAL FINDINGS ON DNA EVIDENCE MISHANDLING

The Supreme Court identified several fundamental flaws in the DNA evidence collection and preservation process that rendered the samples unreliable for forensic analysis:

1. **Faulty Investigation**: Justice Sanjay Karol observed, "A common thread that can be seen to be running through the entire process that has culminated by way of this judgment, is that of faulty investigation"

2. **Procedural Lapses**: The court noted that procedures regarding forensic evidence suggested by various agencies lacked uniformity, creating potential for significant impact on investigated cases

3. **No Eyewitnesses**: The court found that 56 witnesses were examined by the prosecution, yet there were no eyewitnesses to the crime, and the death sentence was based purely on circumstantial evidence that failed to meet the requisite standard of proof

## SUPREME COURT'S COMPREHENSIVE DNA EVIDENCE GUIDELINES

### Collection and Documentation Requirements

The Supreme Court established detailed protocols for DNA sample collection:

- DNA samples must be collected "after due care and compliance of all necessary procedure including swift and appropriate packaging"
- Documentation must include: FIR number and date, sections and statutes involved, details of investigating officer and police station, requisite serial numbers
- Must bear signatures of: medical professional present, investigating officer, independent witnesses
- Absence of independent witnesses shall not compromise evidence collection, but efforts to secure witnesses and reasons for unavailability must be recorded

### Storage and Preservation Protocols

The Supreme Court established stringent requirements recognizing biological materials are "prone to dilution":

- During storage period pending trial and appeal, no package shall be opened, altered, or resealed without express authorization from trial court
- Authorization only granted upon statement from qualified medical professional confirming action will not negatively impact evidence sanctity
- Action must be necessary for proper investigation or trial outcomes

### Chain of Custody Requirements - MOST CRITICAL FOR OUR CASE

**The court directed**: "Right from the point of collection to the logical end, i.e., conviction or acquittal of the accused, a Chain of Custody Register shall be maintained wherein each and every movement of the evidence shall be recorded with counter sign at each end thereof stating also the reason therefor"

**Key Requirements**:
- Chain of Custody Register must be appended as part of trial court record
- Failure to maintain renders investigating officer liable for action
- Addresses common challenges where evidence integrity questioned due to unclear custody chains

## LEGAL FRAMEWORK GOVERNING DNA EVIDENCE

### Constitutional Foundation
- Article 21 of Constitution (right to life and personal liberty) provides foundational framework
- Supreme Court's guidelines represent extension of constitutional mandate
- Ensures scientific evidence serves rather than subverts cause of justice

### Statutory Framework Under Indian Evidence Act
- **Section 45**: Governs expert opinion evidence - "when the Court has to form an opinion upon a point of science, the opinions of persons specially skilled in such science are relevant facts"
- **Kunhiraman vs Manoj**: Established DNA expert reports admissible under Section 45
- **Section 45A**: Addresses electronic evidence and computerized analysis systems

## OUTCOME AND IMPLICATIONS

**Result**: Death sentence overturned, accused acquitted due to forensic evidence being rendered "unusable" due to procedural lapses

**Binding Guidelines Issued**: Court issued binding nationwide directives that fundamentally reshape how biological evidence is managed across India's criminal justice ecosystem

## DIRECT APPLICATION TO STADIUM COLLAPSE CASE

### Parallels with Our Case:
1. **Chain of Custody Failure**: No documented chain of custody for mortar samples
2. **Procedural Lapses**: Haphazard collection without proper protocols
3. **No Representative Present**: Contractor denied right to have representative during sampling
4. **Storage Issues**: Unknown storage and transport conditions
5. **Contamination Risk**: Samples collected in rain/storm conditions

### Legal Arguments Supported:
1. **Inadmissibility**: Forensic evidence without proper chain of custody loses evidentiary value
2. **Constitutional Violation**: Denial of fair procedure violates Article 21
3. **Expert Opinion Challenge**: Improper sampling affects reliability under Section 45
4. **Burden of Proof**: Prosecution must establish evidence reliability beyond reasonable doubt

## COMPENSATION FOR WRONGFUL INCARCERATION

The Supreme Court identified significant gap in India's legal framework regarding compensation for wrongfully incarcerated individuals, noting that Kattavellai had secured a "clean acquittal" after prolonged incarceration, highlighting need for legislative intervention.
